The cost of waiting another quarter

A launch lands on the audience you already built. Not the one you start building launch week.

Nexar's content motion runs through one person, and the most valuable windows on the calendar don't wait for bandwidth. A product launch, a category moment, a raise, a recruiting push: each one converts on the audience that already exists when it arrives. Building that audience takes months. So waiting carries a real price, and you pay it in every window that passes while the audience still isn't there.

Here is what that looks like when it's working. Gal Aga, CEO of Aligned, drives roughly 60% of his company's revenue through LinkedIn. He recently posted the honest version of how:

GA
Gal Aga Co-Founder & CEO at Aligned · 92,000 followers 3w

I have to come clean. LinkedIn is not for all CEOs. It drives ±60% of our ARR. We get 50M+ views/year across internal + Partner/UGC posts. But in Q1, I stopped posting regularly, and leads from LinkedIn dropped 50%.

We're still growing fast, but navigating this was painful.

I've talked a lot about the upside of founder-led growth.

But this is the part we don't talk about enough.

Founder-led growth has a cost.

And most founders underestimate it.

DON'T invest in LinkedIn if you:

1. CAN'T COMMIT TO THE TIME TAX

Writing isn't the hard part. The context switching is. Whenever I post, it steals focus for 60-90min mid-workday. I make final edits, reply to comments, and engage in other posts. Add that to 6-9hr writing/week... Big commitment.

2. DON'T HAVE THE MENTAL BANDWIDTH

You're never "done." Every walk, dinner, or meeting becomes a potential post. The effort isn't the hard part. Staying inspired and excited to write is. And when you stop publishing for a while, restarting feels heavier than starting from zero.

3. CAN'T COMMIT TO CONSISTENCY (FIRST MONTHS, THEN YEARS)

This one hurts most. Momentum is fragile. Miss a few weeks, and the drop is steeper than you expect. In Q1, I posted 50% less. Almost 50% was old content. And I almost didn't engage. My reach crashed, and we had to compensate for it.

4. DON'T TRULY ENJOY BUILDING RELATIONSHIPS (CAN'T SKIP THIS)

Posting is the easy part. What breaks people is everything around it. The replies. The DMs. Showing up in others' feeds. Skip it and your audience feels it immediately. They stop coming back. And so do the leads.

5. NEED CLEAN ATTRIBUTION AND TIDY ROI SPREADSHEETS

It won't show up as last-touch. ~60% of our ARR self-report LinkedIn - but you can't prove the full impact in the CRM right away. Your board will ask. You won't have a clean answer. If you need the dashboard before you commit, you'll quit before it works.

This isn't ads you can treat like a transaction you just pump $$ into.

And it's not PR where you just optimize for the message.

It's trust, built in public.

That takes time and attention.

For me, that's 10+ hours/week.

And having to continuously "growth hack" when things break.

Founder-led growth works.

But it has a cost - and we're not talking about it enough.

Questions buyers actually ask

What you're probably wondering. Plain answers.

I get it's important. But why not wait a quarter?+
Your next product launch is the reason to start now, not the reason to wait. A launch converts on the audience that already exists when it ships. If Zach spends this quarter compounding real followers across the AV and insurance world, the launch lands on a warm crowd. If the profile-building starts launch week, the launch shouts into a room that hasn't arrived yet. Audience is the one marketing asset that compounds, and the compounding can't be bought back later. Starting after the launch costs you the quarter of compounding that the launch needed. The ROI section above has the math on exactly why $3,000 a month pays for itself.
How does the day-to-day actually work?+
Everything runs in one shared Slack channel: you, Zach, and us. Onboarding starts with a short intake form for Zach (eight to ten questions) to fill the gaps in the context graph. From there it's a simple weekly rhythm: every Friday or Sunday, you and Zach see the three posts queued for the coming week in our approval platform. You approve, comment, or ask for a swap; revisions are unlimited, that's the job. Once approved, we post straight to Zach's profile or hand the drafts back, his call. Every week you also get an analytics report covering impressions, follower growth, and ICP relevance. Zach's total time commitment is about five minutes a week.
Why not just have ChatGPT or Claude do this?+
You can. Most LinkedIn content already does. Originality.ai's 2025 study of 3,368 posts from 99 influential profiles flagged 53.7% of long-form LinkedIn content as likely AI-generated. That's exactly the problem. The reason posts hitting Zach's feed feel forgettable is they all read the same. Setting it up in Claude yourself still leaves the hard parts on Itamar's plate: knowing what hooks land on LinkedIn this month, tracking what the AV voices are posting, keeping Zach's voice consistent, and putting in the editing reps. We use AI heavily for the unsexy work (scraping competitor posts, tracking what category leaders publish, keeping your context graph fresh), and real humans on our team write every post. The system gives us category fluency on day one; the humans give you something the algorithm hasn't already seen 10,000 times this week.
Do you handle LinkedIn paid campaigns?+
No, and that's a deliberate call. We focus only on organic, because that's where the real opportunity is for Zach and where we do our best work. Paid amplifies whatever you already have; if the organic profile isn't compounding yet, paid spend just rents reach that vanishes the moment the budget stops. Our honest read: it isn't worth investing in LinkedIn paid until Zach's organic presence is genuinely compounding. Build that first. If paid makes sense after that, we'll happily point you to people in our network who do it well.
Nexar sells dashcams to drivers and data to enterprises. Does LinkedIn reach the buyers that matter?+
LinkedIn isn't for the dashcam buyer. It's for the other side of the business. The AV developers, the insurers, the city DOTs, and the fleet operators who buy Nexar's road-intelligence data are all on LinkedIn, and they read a category's executives before they take a meeting. That's the exact audience Zach's profile is built to reach. The consumer side runs on its own channels. This program is the B2B funnel.
What if Zach doesn't want to post, or hates the drafts?+
Nothing ships without Zach's approval. The five-minute Slack review is a feature, not a workaround. If a draft isn't right, we rewrite. If the system isn't clicking by day 30, we pause and re-scope, no clawback fights. We built the 90-day window so you make the call on real data, not vibes.
How are you different from generic founder-content agencies?+
Most agencies sell engagement metrics. We measure ICP-relevant audience growth. Most agencies write generic operator-life posts in the voice you'd expect. We start from a Context Graph specific to Zach's category, backstory, and the angles Nexar already owns. The cadence is the product, not the volume.
What happens after 90 days?+
If it's working, we keep going (month-to-month, same rate, still no auto-renewal). If it isn't, we shake hands and you keep every post, the voice node, the context graph, and the playbook for running it in-house. The 90 days exist to put that renew-or-walk call on real numbers.
Who owns the IP, the posts, and the context graph?+
Nexar owns everything. Every post, the full Context Graph, the voice node, the 90-day cadence playbook. Walk after the pilot and you walk with all of it. We don't gate it behind continued retainer.
